§ 02

The flour

Most grocery-shelf flour is made in industrial roller mills that separate the wheat kernel into its parts and keep only the endosperm (starch and protein). Removing the bran (fiber, minerals) and the germ (healthy fats, Vitamin E, most of the B vitamins) extends flour's shelf life, but also removes most of the grain's nutrition. The remaining refined flour often gets bleached, and is then "enriched"[1], a legally defined process where a short list of nutrients (typically iron, thiamin, riboflavin, niacin, and folic acid) is added back to hit FDA minimums.[2] Stone-milling, on the other hand, grinds the whole kernel and keeps it in the flour, so there's nothing to add back.

From a sustainability perspective, a handful of modern crops, mainly wheat, rice, corn, and barley, dominate global agriculture, which has narrowed genetic diversity and made the food system more fragile as the climate shifts.[3] Ancient grains generally need less irrigation, fewer synthetic fertilizers, and fewer pesticides than modern varieties, and are more resilient to conditions such as drought, heat, and poor soil.[3] Baking with and consuming them is a small way of keeping older varieties in circulation.

For a long time I went along with the general consensus: whole wheat is healthier but tastes slightly worse. It wasn't until I started milling and baking myself that I understood how much the grain itself shapes how bread tastes and makes you feel.

§ 03

The grain archive

"Ancient grains" are the wheats and cereals that have stayed largely unchanged over the last several hundred years, never bred hard for industrial yield.

Many people also find them easier to digest than modern wheat. The gluten in einkorn, spelt, and emmer has a different protein makeup than modern bread wheat, higher ratios of gliadin to glutenin, and a weaker, less elastic network[4], and studies of einkorn have found lower levels of the peptides most often implicated in wheat sensitivity.[5] When preparing bread with long sourdough fermentation, wild yeast and bacteria pre-digest some of the gluten and break down the phytic acid that can otherwise block mineral absorption.

Einkorn

Triticum monococcum

The only wheat that's never been hybridized, its simpler, weaker gluten and higher protein-to-gluten ratio make it noticeably gentler on digestion. Golden, nutty, and faintly sweet, with a lightness modern wheat traded away for yield.

Domesticated c. 8500 BCE · Fertile Crescent

Spelt

Triticum spelta

A medieval European mainstay whose gluten is unusually water-soluble, easing digestion and helping the body take up more of its B vitamins and protein. Its tough hull and low yield keep it a small-batch grain, mellow, nutty, and warm in the loaf.

Cultivated c. 5000 BCE · Bronze-Age Europe

Rye

Secale cereale

A hardy northern cereal that thrives in poor, cold soil where wheat won't grow, needing little in the way of water or fertilizer. High in fiber and slow to spike blood sugar, it's deep, dark and faintly sour, the natural partner to a long sourdough ferment.

Cultivated c. 1800 BCE · Northern Europe

Sorghum

Sorghum bicolor

A naturally gluten-free staple that still feeds much of the world, prized for surviving drought and heat on a fraction of the water other cereals need. Rich in antioxidants and fiber, it's earthy, faintly sweet, and dense.

Domesticated c. 3000 BCE · Northeast Africa

Millet

Pennisetum glaucum

Tiny, gluten-free, and remarkably drought-tolerant, millet fed entire regions on poor soil long before modern wheat existed. Rich in magnesium and iron with a gentle effect on blood sugar, it's light, faintly corn-sweet, and delicate.

Domesticated c. 2500 BCE · Sahel, West Africa
