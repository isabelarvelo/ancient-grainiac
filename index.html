The baker

Hi, I'm Isa.

Ancient Grainiac is my way of sharing how nourishing and delicious stone-milled ancient grains can be, while supporting small farmers who are building a healthier, more resilient food system.

My first introduction to ancient grains was through regenerative agriculture and the role they play in building soil health and preserving biodiversity. Not only are these crops climate resilient, they also offer a wide variety of health and nutritional benefits, including high-quality proteins, fiber, and powerful antioxidants that protect against inflammation and chronic disease.

For a long time I went along with the consensus that whole wheat is healthier but tastes slightly worse. It wasn't until I started milling and baking with a variety of grains myself that I understood how much the grain itself shapes how bread tastes and how it makes you feel.

— Isa

The flour

Every bake starts with the whole kernel.

Stone Milling

Stone-milled, kernel intact

The whole kernel is ground together, so the nutrient-rich bran and germ stay in the flour. Fiber, minerals, healthy fats, and B vitamins included. Nothing stripped, nothing added back.

Industrial Roller Milling

Roller-milled, then "enriched"

Industrial roller mills separate the kernel into parts and discard most of the bran and germ. What's left is often bleached, then "enriched"1 with a short list of synthetic nutrients to meet FDA minimums.2

Stone-milled flour doesn't need enriching. The nutrition is already there, as nature intended.

The why

Older grains, stronger food system, better bread.

Biodiversity

Every loaf keeps a variety in circulation

Global agriculture has narrowed around a handful of modern crops, reducing genetic diversity and making the food system more fragile as the climate shifts.3 Ancient grains tend to need less water and fewer inputs, and they hold up better in drought, heat, and poor soil.

Digestibility

A gentler kind of gluten

The gluten in einkorn, spelt, and emmer has a different protein makeup, with higher ratios of gliadin to glutenin and a weaker, less elastic network.4 Studies have found lower levels of the peptides most often associated with wheat sensitivity.5

Fermentation

Sourdough does the hard work first

Long sourdough fermentation lets wild yeast and bacteria pre-digest some of the gluten and break down phytic acid that can otherwise block mineral absorption. Slower bread, easier on you.

The grain archive

Five grains. Ten thousand years of farming.

Ancient grains are wheats and cereals that have stayed largely unchanged over the last several hundred years, not bred for industrial yield.

Triticum monococcum

Einkorn

The only wheat that has never been hybridized, einkorn has just 14 chromosomes compared to modern bread wheat's 42. That simpler genetic structure produces a weaker gluten network that many people find easier to digest, along with notably higher levels of lutein, beta-carotene, and protein than any modern wheat variety.
