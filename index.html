§ 02

The flour

Everything I bake is made with stone-milled flour, meaning the whole kernel gets ground together so the nutrient-rich bran and germ stay in the flour rather than being stripped away. Industrial roller mills separate the kernel into its parts and discard most of the bran and germ, taking the fiber, minerals, healthy fats, and B vitamins with them. What's left is often bleached, then "enriched"[1] with a short list of synthetic nutrients to meet FDA minimums.[2]

Stone-milled flour doesn't need enriching. The nutrition is already there, exactly as nature intended.

Consuming ancient grains helps create demand to keep these older varieties in circulation. Global agriculture has narrowed around a handful of modern crops, mainly wheat, rice, corn, and barley, which has reduced genetic diversity and made the food system more fragile as the climate shifts.[3] Ancient grains tend to need less water and fewer inputs than modern varieties, and they hold up better in drought, heat, and poor soil.[3]

For a long time I went along with the general consensus: whole wheat is healthier but tastes slightly worse. It wasn't until I started milling and baking with a variety of grains myself that I understood how much the grain shapes not just how bread tastes, but how it makes you feel.

The grain archive

"Ancient grains" are the wheats and cereals that have stayed largely unchanged over the last several hundred years, not bred for industrial yield.

Many people also find them easier to digest than modern wheat. The gluten in einkorn, spelt, and emmer has a different protein makeup, with higher ratios of gliadin to glutenin and a weaker, less elastic network.[4] Studies of einkorn have also found lower levels of the peptides most often implicated in wheat sensitivity.[5] Long sourdough fermentation allows wild yeast and bacteria to pre-digest some of the gluten and break down phytic acid that can otherwise block mineral absorption.

Einkorn

Triticum monococcum

The only wheat that has never been hybridized, einkorn has just 14 chromosomes compared to modern bread wheat's 42. That simpler genetic structure produces a weaker gluten network that many people find easier to digest, along with notably higher levels of lutein, beta-carotene, and protein than any modern wheat variety. It has a buttery and malty flavor.

Domesticated c. 8500 BCE · Fertile Crescent

Spelt

Triticum spelta

Spelt is genetically closer to modern bread wheat than other ancient grains, making it the most approachable to bake with. Its gluten is more water-soluble than modern wheat's, which is thought to make it easier to digest and its minerals more bioavailable. It was a staple of central Europe for centuries and remains widely used in German baking today.

Domesticated c. 5000 BCE · Near East and Caucasus

Rye

Secale cereale

Rye contains less gluten than wheat but far more pentosans, water-absorbing carbohydrates that give rye doughs their characteristic stickiness and help finished loaves stay moist longer. It is exceptionally high in fiber, has a lower glycemic index than most grains, and ferments faster than any other flour in a sourdough culture. The flavor is earthy and complex.

Cultivated c. 6600 BCE · South-central Turkey

Sorghum

Sorghum bicolor

Sorghum is one of the five most important cereal crops in the world and the most important grain in sub-Saharan Africa, yet it is almost unknown in American kitchens. It is naturally gluten-free and is rich in B vitamins, iron, magnesium, and fiber. Sorghum is highly versatile and can be cooked like rice, ground into flour, popped, or made into syrup.

Domesticated c. 3000 BCE · Eastern Sudan

Millet

Pennisetum glaucum

Pearl millet is the most widely grown millet in the world and a dietary staple across the Sahel, India, and parts of South Asia. It needs as little as a third of the water that wheat requires and produces reliable yields on soils too poor and sandy for any other grain. It is gluten-free and notably high in iron, magnesium, phosphorus, and zinc, with a lower glycemic index than most cereals.

Domesticated c. 2500 BCE · Sahel, West Africa
