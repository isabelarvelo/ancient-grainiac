§ 02

The grain archive

"Ancient grains" are the wheats and cereals that have stayed close to their wild ancestors — never bred for industrial yield, so they kept their flavor, their quirks, and their deep nutrition. Here's what I bake with, and roughly when humans first did the same.

Einkorn

Triticum monococcum

The original cultivated wheat — a single grain per spikelet. Golden, nutty, gently sweet. Low, delicate gluten, so it bakes into tender, close-crumbed loaves.

Domesticated c. 8500 BCE · Fertile Crescent

Emmer

Triticum dicoccum

The staple grain of ancient Egypt and the bread of the pyramids. Earthy and rustic with a faint sweetness — heartier and more structured than einkorn.

Domesticated c. 8000 BCE · Near East

Spelt

Triticum spelta

A medieval European mainstay with a mellow, nutty warmth. Its gluten is more water-soluble, giving an open, easygoing dough that many find gentler to digest.

Cultivated c. 5000 BCE · Bronze-Age Europe

Khorasan

Triticum turanicum

Enormous amber kernels, twice the size of modern wheat. Rich, buttery and almost golden-tasting — luxurious in an enriched dough or a slow-baked crust.

Ancient origin · Greater Iran / Fertile Crescent

Rye

Secale cereale

Not a wheat at all — a hardy northern cereal that thrives where others fail. Deep, dark and faintly sour; the natural partner to a long sourdough ferment.

Cultivated c. 1800 BCE · Northern Europe
