The baker

Hi, I'm Isa and I love ancient grains!

My first introduction to ancient grains was through regenerative agriculture and the role they play in building soil health and preserving biodiversity. Not only are these crops climate resilient, they also offer a wide variety of health and nutritional benefits, including high-quality proteins, fiber, and powerful antioxidants that protect against inflammation and chronic disease. And best of all, they are delicious!

Ancient Grainiac is my way of sharing the goodness of these grains with you. I hope you can experience how nourishing and delicious stone-milled ancient grains can be, while supporting small farmers who are building a healthier, more resilient food system.

The flour

Every bake starts with the whole kernel.

Stone Milling

Stone-milled, kernel intact

The whole kernel is ground together, so the nutrient-rich bran and germ stay in the flour. Fiber, minerals, healthy fats, and B vitamins included. Nothing stripped, nothing added back.

Industrial Roller Milling

Roller-milled, then "enriched"

Industrial roller mills separate the kernel into parts and discard most of the bran and germ. What's left is often bleached, then "enriched"1 with a short list of synthetic nutrients to meet FDA minimums.2

Stone-milled flour doesn't need enriching. The nutrition is already there, as nature intended.

The why

Older grains, stronger food system, better bread.

Biodiversity

Every loaf keeps a variety in circulation

Global agriculture has narrowed around a handful of modern crops, reducing genetic diversity and making the food system more fragile as the climate shifts.3 Ancient grains tend to need less water and fewer inputs, and they hold up better in drought, heat, and poor soil.3

Digestibility

A gentler kind of gluten

The gluten in einkorn, spelt, and emmer has a different protein makeup, with higher ratios of gliadin to glutenin and a weaker, less elastic network.4 Studies of einkorn have found lower levels of the peptides most often associated with wheat sensitivity.5

Fermentation

Sourdough does the hard work first

Long sourdough fermentation lets wild yeast and bacteria pre-digest some of the gluten and break down phytic acid that can otherwise block mineral absorption. Slower bread, easier on you.

For a long time I went along with the consensus that whole wheat is healthier but tastes slightly worse. It wasn't until I started milling and baking with a variety of grains myself that I understood how much the grain itself shapes how bread tastes and how it makes you feel.

The grain archive

"Ancient grains" are wheats and cereals that have stayed largely unchanged over the last several hundred years, not bred for industrial yield.

Einkorn

Triticum monococcum

The only wheat that has never been hybridized, einkorn has just 14 chromosomes compared to modern bread wheat's 42. That simpler genetic structure produces a weaker gluten network that many people find easier to digest, along with notably higher levels of lutein, beta-carotene, and protein than any modern wheat variety. It has a buttery and malty flavor.

Domesticated c. 8500 BCE · Fertile Crescent

Spelt

Triticum spelta

Spelt is genetically closer to modern bread wheat than other ancient grains, making it the most approachable to bake with. Its gluten is more water-soluble than modern wheat's, which is thought to make it easier to digest and its minerals more bioavailable. It was a staple of central Europe for centuries and remains widely used in German baking today.

Domesticated c. 5000 BCE · Near East and Caucasus

Rye

Secale cereale

Rye contains less gluten than wheat but far more pentosans, water-absorbing carbohydrates that give rye doughs their characteristic stickiness and help finished loaves stay moist longer. It is high in fiber, has a lower glycemic index than most grains, and ferments faster than any other flour in a sourdough culture. The flavor is earthy and complex.

Cultivated c. 6600 BCE · South-central Turkey

Sorghum

Sorghum bicolor

Sorghum is one of the five most important cereal crops in the world and the most important grain in sub-Saharan Africa, yet it is almost unknown in American kitchens. It is naturally gluten-free and is rich in B vitamins, iron, magnesium, and fiber. Sorghum is highly versatile and can be cooked like rice, ground into flour, popped, or made into syrup.

Domesticated c. 3000 BCE · Eastern Sudan

Millet

Pennisetum glaucum

Pearl millet is the most widely grown millet in the world and a dietary staple across the Sahel, India, and parts of South Asia. It needs as little as a third of the water that wheat requires and produces reliable yields on soils too poor and sandy for any other grain. It is gluten-free and notably high in iron, magnesium, phosphorus, and zinc, with a lower glycemic index than most cereals.

Domesticated c. 2500 BCE · Sahel, West Africa
